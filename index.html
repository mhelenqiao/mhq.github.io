Bio

I am a PhD candidate in the Causal Cognition Lab in the Experimental Psychology Department at University College London (UCL). My research investigates judgment and decision-making, Bayesian and causal reasoning, coherence-based reasoning, and AI alignment, with a particular focus on reasoning in legal contexts. Using cognitive science and experimental methods, I investigate how people and large language models evaluate evidence and reach judgments, and I develop Bayesian and computational cognitive models to capture these processes.

I am always keen to connect, collaborate, and learn. Reach me at helen.qiao.18@ucl.ac.uk if you'd like to talk about legal psychology, AI, philosophy, human cognition, or anything adjacent and beyond.

Projects

Bayesian and Coherence-Based Models of Legal Reasoning

A unified model reconciling Bayesian inference with coherence-based reasoning to explain how jurors evaluate evidence and reach verdicts, in collaboration with Keith Holyoak (UCLA).

Bayesian and Coherence-Based Models of Legal Reasoning
Chain Event Graphs for Narrative Legal Reasoning

A normative framework formalising the role of stories in legal decision-making using Chain Event Graphs, in collaboration with statistician James Smith (University of Warwick).

Chain Event Graphs for Narrative Legal Reasoning
Probabilistic Belief Updating in Legal Decision-Making

An empirical investigation of how people revise beliefs in response to evidence.

Probabilistic Belief Updating in Legal Decision-Making
AI Alignment of Legal Decision-Making

Examining how AI systems can be aligned with human legal reasoning across probabilistic, binary, and argumentation-based decision frameworks, in collaboration with computer scientists from Utrecht University and the University of Groningen.

AI Alignment of Legal Decision-Making
Causal Responsibility Attribution in Human and AI Agency

Investigating how people assign moral and legal responsibility in causal chains involving human actors and AI agents, in collaboration with the University of Utah and LSE.

Causal Responsibility Attribution in Human and AI Agency
Prior Beliefs and Evidential Reasoning Across Crime Types

Investigating how pre-existing beliefs and prior expectations shape the way people evaluate evidence across different categories of criminal cases, in collaboration with Glasgow Caledonian University.

Prior Beliefs and Evidential Reasoning Across Crime Types
Non-Deceptive Editing and Evidence Credibility

Investigating how non-deceptive edits to user-generated evidence affect perceptions of its credibility, reliability, and admissibility in court, in collaboration with Queen's University Belfast.

Non-Deceptive Editing and Evidence Credibility
